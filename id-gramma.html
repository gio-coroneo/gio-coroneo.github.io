Giovanni Coroneo

ID gramma:
Faces of technology.

( Summary )An experimental project that misuses Apple's Face ID the way Bruno Munari once misused the Xerox scanner. By scanning and translating into image what the sensor believes it recognises, the work produces a series of digital sculptures, later shown at MEET in Milan.

( Scope )( Experimental Tech, 3D Design )

( Context )Digital Image Culture Course, MSc in Communication Design, Politecnico di Milano.

Work About Play CV^

( Starting Point )In his Xerografie, Bruno Munari used the photocopier against
its purpose, moving objects mid-scan to make images it was never built to produce.
ID gramma asks how that spirit of misuse stays relevant with technologies we carry.

Face ID exists to confirm one thing: that a specific face is the right face. Here it turns from recognition to image-making. Fed surfaces it reads as a face, its interpretations become form. The project begins not from a problem but from curiosity.

( Digital Sculptures )What the sensor returns is neither a portrait nor a scan, but something in between: a series of digital sculptures born from the gap between
what the machine expects and what it is actually given.

A recognition tool, designed to draw what it could previously only recognise.

( At MEET )The sculptures were shown at MEET, Milan's centre for digital culture, where the distorted faces stood as standalone exhibits rather than failed scans.
We then built the visual identity for the event's launch, starting from the logo.

The logo follows the same deconstruction logic as the object. Just as the heads reveal their form only from a privileged angle, so does the logotype, exploded in Blender
into a cloud of micro-elements that realign into "ID gramma".

( CAMPAIGN )For the launch we built a poster campaign that turns the whole operation into a visual language: details of the scanned 3D subjects become recurring background patterns, while the layout follows MEET's rules without losing its own identity.
